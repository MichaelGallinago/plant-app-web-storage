Fertilizers for Houseplants

Houseplants need fertilizers at each stage of development. A lack of nutrients can negatively affect flowering, growth, and the overall appearance of the plants. You can find various complex fertilizers in flower shops. However, high-quality fertilizers can also be made at home using readily available materials.

Feeding Houseplants

Timely application of fertilizers to potted houseplants is the key to proper development, healthy growth, and abundant flowering. The reason is that the potting mix in a flower pot contains only a limited supply of specific nutrients, which depletes over time. If the plants are not fed at least twice a month, they will begin to starve. This will inevitably lead to a decline in the plant’s decorative appeal.

You can make your own nutrient mix at home, using various organic waste such as eggshells, onion skins, coffee grounds, banana peels, aquarium water, used tea, etc. Fertilizers made from organic materials actively enrich the substrate with beneficial nutrients, help disinfect it, and promote the colonization of useful microorganisms.

When to Feed

Houseplants may require varying amounts of micro- and macroelements throughout the year.

Spring

Spring is the time for the awakening of all plants, and houseplants are no exception. During this time, their growth accelerates, and buds start to appear. Therefore, spring is when plants especially need feeding. Feed them once every 2–3 weeks until the bush begins to bloom. At the beginning of spring, plants need feedings with a high nitrogen content. Just before flowering, their need for potassium and phosphorus increases.

Note! Liquid nutrients should not be applied to dry soil. Be sure to water the plants well before feeding.

Summer

In summer, flowers also need regular feeding, as they expend a lot of nutrients and energy on blooming. During this time, they especially need potassium. It is recommended to feed them twice a month.

When performing foliar feeding, avoid letting the nutrient solution come into contact with the flowers. This can lead to the formation of brownish spots, which reduces the decorative appeal of the flowers.

Autumn

As autumn arrives, it is recommended to significantly reduce the amount of watering and frequency of feeding. Otherwise, the flowers will not be able to properly prepare for the dormant period. In autumn, houseplants especially need phosphorus.

Feedings should be done once every 4 weeks, and the summer dosage should be reduced by half. However, there is an exception to this rule — houseplants that bloom in the autumn. In this case, the feeding schedule should remain the same as in summer. Specialists recommend using granular fertilizers at this time, which have a prolonged effect.

Winter

During the cold winter months, almost all houseplants are in a dormant state. During this time, they should be kept cool. At this time, the plants not only rest but also form buds.

Feeding is not done during this period, and watering is also reduced. However, there is an exception — plants that bloom in winter. These plants should be fed once every 15 days with fertilizers that contain phosphorus and potassium.

Homemade Fertilizers

Experienced gardeners who have been growing houseplants for years have many recipes for "homemade fertilizers," made from the simplest ingredients. These eco-friendly and effective feedings are sure to "please" all houseplants.

Sugar

Almost all houseplants love sugar. It contains only glucose and fructose. Glucose gives the plant a burst of energy and ensures better absorption of organic compounds. However, for glucose to be well absorbed, an optimal amount of carbon dioxide is necessary. If there is not enough of this element in the substrate, the feeding can lead to mold formation.

To prevent this, it is recommended to use EM preparations. Specialists advise using pharmaceutical glucose or lump sugar for feeding. For every liter of water, one glucose tablet or one lump of sugar is used. Such feedings should not be done more than once every 30 days.

Yeast

Yeast is quite popular in indoor gardening. This is because it contains many hormones involved in regulating cell division. Yeast is also rich in thiamine, B vitamins, and auxin.

Thanks to yeast, the process of mineralizing organic matter occurs much faster. It also helps produce essential macroelements and improves the activity of beneficial microorganisms in the soil mix. The composition of yeast is very similar to that of complex mineral fertilizers.

To prepare the nutrient solution, dissolve 1 gram of yeast and 1.5 tablespoons of sugar in half a liter of warm water. Feedings should be done no more than once every four weeks. The fertilizer can be used for both decorative-leaved and flowering plants.

Coffee Grounds

This type of fertilizer is perfect for plants that prefer slightly acidic soil. Used coffee helps lower the pH. If the grounds are thoroughly dried and mixed with the substrate during repotting or planting, it improves the soil's looseness and drainage qualities. This fertilizer not only enriches the soil with nutrients but also improves its quality and positively impacts the beneficial microbiota.

Tea Leaves

Plants can also be fed with used tea leaves, which should first be thoroughly dried. It has a pronounced nutritional effect and helps improve the structure of the soil mix. Typically, dried tea is mixed into the soil, or it can be added to the new container during repotting. The dry used tea should constitute no more than a third of the total amount of substrate.

House violets particularly love tea leaf feedings. After such treatments, the buds form much faster, and the flowering duration is increased. In addition, the used tea can be used as mulch on the surface of the soil in the pot, which slows down water evaporation.

Bread

Stale bread can also be used as a fertilizer for flowers. Cut it into small pieces and leave it outdoors until it dries completely. To prepare the nutrient solution, soak the breadcrumbs in warm water. The container should be tightly covered with a lid, and a weight should be placed on top. The mixture should stand for at least 7 days.

To make the mixture more nutritious, add freshly picked weeds. After a week, strain the mixture. Plants are watered with it twice a month, no more frequently. But before feeding, remember to dilute the nutrient solution with water in a 1:10 ratio. This fertilizer is suitable for all indoor plants.

Banana Peel

There are various recipes with banana peels, but the most popular one is an infusion. To make it, take fresh banana peels, cut them into small pieces, and place them in a glass jar. Then pour freshly boiled water over them. The infusion will be ready after 24 hours. Strain the infusion and dilute it with clean water (3:1). Use this solution to fertilize plants once every two weeks.

Well-dried banana peels should be ground into a powder. Then, mix it with the substrate to improve its structure. The peels are rich in potassium, which is especially needed by flowering plants in the spring and summer months. This fertilizer will help the plant gain strength for more vibrant and longer-lasting flowering.

Citrus Peel

The peels of mandarins, grapefruits, oranges, and lemons contain many vitamins. Therefore, they enrich the substrate with nutrients and help disinfect it. To make the infusion, combine freshly boiled water with fresh peels. After half a day, the fertilizer will be ready.

Strained infusion can be used to fertilize plants through root application. This infusion can also be used to mist the bushes with a spray bottle. It helps fight various diseases and pests (including spider mites).

Eggshells

Eggshells are rich in calcium. To create a nutrient-rich infusion, crush the eggshells and place them in a jar, then pour water over them (use the shells of six eggs for 1 liter of water). Cover the jar with cheesecloth. Let the mixture sit in a warm place for 1 week.

Dilute the infusion with clean water (10:1). Use the prepared fertilizer to feed your houseplants. It is especially beneficial for plants that react negatively to soil acidification.

Onion Skin

Onion skins can be used as both a fertilizer and a pest control remedy. Additionally, this type of feeding can be used for all indoor plants.

To make a useful decoction, combine 0.5 liters of water with 15 grams of onion skin. Boil the mixture over low heat for 15 minutes, then let it steep for four hours. Strain the decoction and use it to treat bushes with a spray bottle, or apply it for root fertilization.

Potato Decoction

After potatoes are boiled, the water in the pot becomes rich in nutrients. This water can also be used to fertilize flowers. In addition, the starch from the potatoes provides the plants with energy.

This type of fertilizer positively influences the development of the root system. Once the decoction from boiling the potatoes has cooled, dilute it with water (1:3). Be sure the decoction is not salty. Decorative plants with thick, fleshy leaves respond very well to this type of feeding.

Aloe Vera Juice

Aloe vera juice has a pronounced antimicrobial effect. It also strengthens the plant's immune system, promotes growth, activates regenerative processes in damaged tissues, and fills the plant with vitality.

The juice is obtained from the leaves of the aloe plant, which should be at least four years old. Cut a few leaf blades, place them in a plastic bag, and store them on a shelf in the refrigerator. After 7 days, they will release the maximum amount of juice. Pass them through a meat grinder, then squeeze out the juice. Dilute ½ teaspoon of juice in 0.5 liters of water. Water the plants twice a week with this solution.

Cigarette Ash (Wood Ash)

Cigarette ash can be used as a mineral fertilizer, just like wood ash. For 1 cup of water (250 ml), take ½ tablespoon of ash (wood ash). Use this mixture to fertilize plants twice a month.

This nutrient solution contains phosphorus and potassium, which are necessary for plants during the bud formation and blooming processes. It is also recommended to feed flowers in the autumn to help them survive the winter. Cyclamens, fuchsias, begonias, and geraniums particularly respond well to this type of feeding.

Aquarium Water

Aquarium water is a source of many nutrients that improve the development and stimulate the growth of houseplants. It is recommended for fertilizing from the beginning of spring until the second half of summer.

This water helps normalize the pH of the soil mix and encourages beneficial microorganisms to populate the substrate. It also improves the quality of the soil. Since it contains a lot of nitrogen, plants wake up faster from dormancy and start growing actively.

Pharmacy Fertilizers

For preparing fertilizers for houseplants, you can also use budget-friendly products from pharmacies, which can be found in almost every home. Some of these substances are quite potent and should be used only once a year or even less frequently.

Tooth Powder (Paste)

Tooth powder has long been used for fertilizing flowers, not just for cleaning teeth. To make the fertilizer, combine 0.5 liters of water with 8 tablespoons of wood ash and tooth powder, and 4 teaspoons of copper sulfate. Mix thoroughly until all components dissolve completely.

First, carefully loosen the substrate around the roots. Soak the stems and roots with the mixture. After this, do not water the substrate in the pot for a week. Experienced gardeners recommend placing the plant in a dark, cool place during this time. During this period, the roots will fully recover from rot.

Succinic Acid

Succinic acid is available in tablets and is freely sold in every pharmacy. This substance actively stimulates plant growth. It is also used to revive a severely damaged plant that is on the brink of dying.

In addition, this acid is a source of vitamins and trace elements. For root feeding, use a solution made from 5 liters of water and ½ tablet. This type of feeding lasts for up to 12 months.

Castor Oil

Houseplants are also fed with castor oil. It helps the plant form a large number of buds. Plants are fed with castor oil from March to the second half of July.

To prepare a nutrient solution, mix ½ teaspoon of the substance with 0.5 liters of water. This solution is applied to the substrate twice a month. Note that a fresh mixture should be prepared for each feeding.

Iodine

Iodine is a chemical element that does not occur in its pure form in nature. Usually, it is part of a complex compound. However, iodine is very important for houseplants. It enhances plant growth, increases the abundance of flowering, and provides protection against certain diseases. Iodine also helps repel some pests.

Iodine is needed by all plants grown indoors. This element is typically included in most mineral complexes. It is also used as a preventive measure against powdery mildew, fungal diseases, and to strengthen the plant's immune system. To make a nutrient solution, combine 1 ml of the iodine solution with 350 ml of water. Use the mixture for spraying the plants, feeding adult flowers, and disinfecting seeds.

Ammonia Solution

Ammonia contains a large amount of nitrogen, which is easily absorbed by houseplants. It gives them a boost of energy and vitality.

To prepare a nutrient solution, combine 0.5 liters of water with ½ tablespoon of ammonia solution. Changes will be noticeable almost immediately after the feeding: the leaves will become stronger, their color will intensify, and the budding process will accelerate. This mixture also helps eliminate harmful microorganisms in the soil.

Ascorbic Acid

Ascorbic acid is used for fertilizing plants that grow in acidic soil mixtures, such as azaleas, hydrangeas, and camellias. Regular root feeding with an ascorbic acid solution (0.5 liters of water with ½ tablet) will ensure their proper development and healthy growth.

For other houseplants, it is recommended to use a nutrient mixture made from 0.5 liters of water, ½ tablet of ascorbic acid, and 0.5 tablespoon each of sugar and dry yeast. The infusion will be ready in a day. Dilute the mixture with clean water in a 10:1 ratio.

To treat a plant affected by chlorosis, mix 2 liters of water, 50 ascorbic acid tablets, and ½ teaspoon of copper sulfate. The solution should be diluted with water in a 1:5 ratio. Use this solution regularly every 8-12 days.

Potassium Permanganate

Potassium permanganate contains a large amount of manganese and potassium, which are essential for the proper development and healthy growth of houseplants. However, potassium permanganate can acidify the soil, so it is mostly used in nutrient mixtures for begonias, hydrangeas, and tradescantias.

This solution has strong disinfectant properties and is also used to fight pests. There is no specific dosage when preparing the nutrient solution. The concentration depends on the plant species, its condition, and the deficiency of active micro- and macro-elements. The universal solution has a pinkish color and is used once every 30 days.
